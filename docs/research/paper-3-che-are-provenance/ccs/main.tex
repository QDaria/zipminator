\begin{abstract}
Composing entropy from heterogeneous sources---quantum hardware,
WiFi Channel State Information (CSI), and operating system random
number generators---is essential for post-quantum cryptographic
systems but lacks formal provenance guarantees.
%
We present the Certified Heterogeneous Entropy (\che) framework,
which makes three contributions.
%
First, we introduce Algebraic Randomness Extraction (\are), a new
family of seeded extractors parameterized by algebraic programs over
bounded number domains with six arithmetic operations, generated
deterministically from SHAKE-256.
%
The core construction operates over five classical domains
($\N_n, \Z_n, \Q_n, \R_n, \CC_n$) and extends to quaternions
($\mathbb{H}$, non-commutative), octonions ($\mathbb{O}$,
non-associative), finite fields $\mathrm{GF}(p^n)$ (provable
per-step uniformity), and $p$-adic numbers.
%
Second, we describe a certified composition protocol that XOR-fuses
entropy from multiple independent sources while constructing
Merkle-tree provenance certificates proving per-source contribution.
%
Third, we show that the framework degrades gracefully when individual
sources fail, maintaining accurate min-entropy bounds without silent
fallback.
%
Our implementation processes 6.8\,MB of IBM Quantum entropy (156
qubits), WiFi CSI entropy from ESP32-S3 hardware, and OS entropy
through the \are extractor and compositor pipeline.
%
NIST SP~800-90B testing validates output quality.
%
The provenance certificates provide a complete audit trail satisfying
DORA Art.~7 requirements for cryptographic key lifecycle management
in EU-regulated financial institutions.
%
We formally prove that GF-domain steps preserve min-entropy
exactly (bijection on the multiplicative group) and show
empirically that the two-layer architecture (algebraic fold +
SHA-256 expansion) provides defense-in-depth extraction
distinct from hash-only approaches.
%
We identify what is proven, what is conjectured, and what
remains open, providing a clear roadmap for the extractor
theory community.
%
All code is open-source.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% Paragraph 1: The problem
Post-quantum cryptographic systems such as
ML-KEM-768~\cite{nist2024fips203} require high-quality entropy for
key generation, but production environments rarely depend on a single
entropy source.
%
Quantum random number generators (QRNGs) provide information-theoretic
guarantees~\cite{ma2016qrng} but are expensive and intermittently
available.
%
Classical hardware sources such as WiFi CSI~\cite{anon2026csi}
offer abundant, zero-marginal-cost entropy but with weaker guarantees.
%
Operating system PRNGs (\texttt{os.urandom}) are ubiquitous but
opaque.
%
Our prior work on quantum-certified
anonymization~\cite{anon2026quantum} and unilateral CSI
entropy~\cite{anon2026csi} demonstrated the need for
multi-source entropy composition.
%
In practice, cryptographic systems must compose entropy from
whichever sources are available, yet no existing framework provides
cryptographic proof of which sources contributed to a given key's
entropy, nor faithfully adjusted degradation bounds when sources fail.

% Paragraph 2: Why this matters (DORA, regulatory)
The European Union's Digital Operational Resilience Act
(DORA)~\cite{dora2022}, effective since 1 July 2025, requires
financial entities to document cryptographic policies, manage key
lifecycles (Art.~7), and respond to cryptographic developments
including quantum threats (Art.~6.4).
%
A provenance certificate that answers ``which entropy sources
contributed to this key, and what was their health status at
generation time?'' directly addresses these regulatory requirements.

% Paragraph 3: Our contributions
We present the Certified Heterogeneous Entropy (\che) framework with
four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Algebraic Randomness Extraction (\are).}
    A new family of algebraic pre-conditioners operating over nine
    bounded number domains ($\N, \Z, \Q, \R, \CC, \mathbb{H},
    \mathbb{O}, \mathrm{GF}(p^n), \Q_p$) with six arithmetic
    operations, composed with SHA-256 for a two-layer extraction
    architecture.
    %
    Unlike hash-based extractors
    (Trevisan~\cite{trevisan2001extractors},
    GUV~\cite{guruswami2009unbalanced}, leftover hash
    lemma~\cite{impagliazzo1989one}), \are provides seed-parameterized
    algebraic mixing as defense in depth.
    %
    We prove per-step uniformity for the GF domain
    (\Cref{thm:gf-uniform}) and identify the general extraction
    bound as an open problem.

  \item \textbf{Certified composition with Merkle provenance.}
    An XOR-based composition protocol where each entropy contribution
    is recorded in a provenance record with canonical encoding.
    %
    A SHA-256 Merkle tree over these records yields a single root hash
    that cryptographically binds the composition to its constituent
    sources.

  \item \textbf{Graceful degradation with accurate bounds.}
    When individual sources fail, the compositor excludes them,
    adjusts the min-entropy estimate downward, and records the
    degradation in the provenance certificate.
    %
    No silent fallback occurs.

  \item \textbf{DORA Art.~7 compliance.}
    The provenance certificates serve as audit artifacts answering
    regulatory queries about cryptographic key provenance, source
    health, and entropy quality at the time of generation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

% 2.1 Seeded Randomness Extractors
\subsection{Seeded Randomness Extractors}
\label{sec:related:extractors}

Seeded randomness extractors transform weakly random sources into
nearly uniform output using a short uniform seed.
%
Trevisan's extractor~\cite{trevisan2001extractors} achieves
near-optimal seed length via combinatorial designs.
%
Doron and Ribeiro~\cite{doron2024linear} recently gave the first
truly linear-time instantiation of Trevisan's extractor in the RAM
model with optimal seed length $O(\log(n/\varepsilon))$.
%
The GUV construction~\cite{guruswami2009unbalanced} based on
Parvaresh--Vardy codes achieves better parameters for unbalanced
extractors; Goodman, Li, and
Zuckerman~\cite{goodman2024condensers} extended the GUV paradigm to
construct the first explicit condenser for Chor--Goldreich sources
with arbitrary block length and entropy rate (FOCS 2024).
%
Golowich~\cite{golowich2024expanders} gave simpler constant-degree
lossless expanders with improved parameters over the
Capalbo--Reingold--Vadhan--Wigderson construction (SODA 2024).

The leftover hash lemma (LHL)~\cite{impagliazzo1989one} guarantees
extraction via universal hash
families~\cite{carter1979universal}.
%
Recent work extends LHL to cyclotomic
rings~\cite{boudgoust2025lhl} and modules over number
fields~\cite{albrecht2025gaussian} for post-quantum lattice
cryptography (PKC 2025).
%
Chattopadhyay and Goodman~\cite{chattopadhyay2025leakage} showed
that only three independent sources suffice for leakage-resilient
extraction (STOC 2025).

In practical QRNG post-processing, Foreman
\etal~\cite{foreman2024cryptomite} provide the Cryptomite library
implementing Toeplitz and two-source extractors, while Dang
\etal~\cite{dang2025tches} achieve 42~Gbps Toeplitz extraction on
FPGA.
%
Foreman and Masanes~\cite{foreman2025seedless} introduced the first
seedless extractor for device-independent quantum cryptography.

None of these constructions use algebraic programs over heterogeneous
number domains.
%
A systematic search of arXiv, IACR ePrint, and Semantic Scholar
(2024--April 2026) found zero prior work defining extractors as
programs that operate across the number-domain tower
$\N \to \Z \to \Q \to \R \to \CC$.
%
Existing algebraic extractors are confined to single domains:
GF($2^n$) for Raz-based constructions, $\Z_p$ for NTT-based
extractors~\cite{foreman2024cryptomite}, and single group
structures for group-action extractors~\cite{jiang2025group}.
%
\are occupies a distinct, previously unexplored point in the
extractor design space.


% 2.2 Multi-Source Entropy Composition
\subsection{Multi-Source Entropy Composition}
\label{sec:related:composition}

XOR composition of independent sources is the standard approach for
defense-in-depth entropy: if at least one source is uncompromised, the
output retains at least the strongest source's min-entropy.
%
Dinur~\cite{dinur2024xor} proved tight indistinguishability bounds
for XOR of independent random permutations via Fourier analysis
(EUROCRYPT 2024).
%
Grujic and Verbauwhede~\cite{grujic2024linear} derived tight output
min-entropy bounds for linear correctors used in TRNG
post-processing (IEEE TIFS 2024), achieving up to 130\% higher
extraction efficiency than prior bounds.

Hardware multi-source designs have advanced: Lou
\etal~\cite{lou2025qpp} demonstrated a cross-platform software TRNG
harvesting entropy from CPU pipeline timing, DRAM refresh, and cache
miss jitter, achieving 7.85--7.95~$\bpb$ across
Windows/macOS/Raspberry~Pi.
%
However, no existing system combines quantum, WiFi CSI, and OS
entropy with cryptographic provenance of the composition.


% 2.3 Entropy Provenance and Certification
\subsection{Entropy Provenance and Certification}
\label{sec:related:provenance}

Certified quantum randomness has seen breakthrough results.
%
Aaronson \etal~\cite{aaronson2025certified} demonstrated the first
certified randomness from a quantum processor: 71,313 certifiably
random bits from the 56-qubit Quantinuum H2-1, verified by
1.1~exaFLOPS of classical computation
(Nature, 2025).
%
Amer \etal~\cite{amer2025applications} surveyed applications of
certified randomness in cryptography, differential privacy, and
finance (Nature Reviews Physics, 2025).
%
These results establish quantum-only provenance via computational
hardness assumptions.
%
Renner's group has also shown that causally independent processes
produce extractable randomness from correlated
inputs~\cite{renner2025causal}.
%
Coretti \etal~\cite{coretti2025multisource} studied multi-source
extraction in the random oracle model, while Jiang
\etal~\cite{jiang2025group} introduced group-action-based extractors
for commitment schemes (Eurocrypt 2025).

Our approach differs from all of these: we provide
\emph{multi-source} provenance via Merkle-tree
certificates~\cite{merkle1989certified} that record which
heterogeneous sources contributed to a composition, with what health
status, and at what time.
%
This is complementary to quantum certification; it answers a
different question (``which sources contributed?'' rather than ``was
this generated by a quantum process?'').


% 2.4 Regulatory Frameworks
\subsection{Regulatory Frameworks for Cryptographic Entropy}
\label{sec:related:regulatory}

NIST completed the SP~800-90 series with SP~800-90C
(final, September 2025)~\cite{nist2025sp80090c}, specifying four RBG
construction classes that combine DRBG mechanisms with entropy
sources.
%
NIST and BSI jointly published IR~8446
(January 2026)~\cite{nist2026ir8446}, bridging the SP~800-90 series
and BSI AIS~20/31.
%
BSI released AIS~20/31 version~3.0 (September
2024)~\cite{bsi2024ais31}, a major revision introducing
min-entropy claims for PTG.3 functionality class sources.
%
Aslan \etal~\cite{aslan2025observations} showed that the compression
and collision estimators in SP~800-90B tend to underestimate entropy,
while Markov and prediction estimators are more accurate.

The EU's Digital Operational Resilience Act
(DORA)~\cite{dora2022}, effective since 17~January 2025 (Norway:
1~July 2025), requires financial entities to document encryption
policies (Art.~6.1), update cryptographic technology based on
cryptanalysis developments including quantum threats (Art.~6.4), and
manage cryptographic key lifecycles (Art.~7).
%
The implicit PQC migration mandate in Art.~6.4 creates a commercial
need for entropy infrastructure that is both quantum-ready and
auditable.

\subsection{Min-Entropy and Randomness Extractors}
\label{sec:prelim:extractors}

\begin{definition}[Min-Entropy]
The min-entropy of a discrete random variable $X$ is
$\minentropy(X) = -\log_2 \max_x \Pr[X = x]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Seeded Extractor]
A function $\mathrm{Ext}: \{0,1\}^n \times \{0,1\}^d \to \{0,1\}^m$
is a $(k, \varepsilon)$-extractor if for every distribution $X$ on
$\{0,1\}^n$ with $\minentropy(X) \geq k$, the distribution
$\mathrm{Ext}(X, U_d)$ is $\varepsilon$-close to uniform in
statistical distance, where $U_d$ is uniform on $\{0,1\}^d$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{SHAKE-256 and SHA-256}
\label{sec:prelim:shake}

% Brief description of SHAKE-256 XOF and SHA-256
SHAKE-256 is a NIST-standardized~\cite{nist2015sha3} extendable-output
function (XOF) based on Keccak.
%
SHA-256 is a collision-resistant hash function from the SHA-2 family.
%
Both are used as building blocks: SHAKE-256 for deterministic program
generation, SHA-256 for Merkle-tree construction and output expansion.

\section{Algebraic Randomness Extraction}
\label{sec:are}

\subsection{Overview}
\label{sec:are:overview}

An \are extractor is defined by a \emph{program} $\mathcal{P} =
(s_1, \ldots, s_k)$, where each step $s_i = (d_i, v_i, w_i, o_i)$
specifies:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $d_i \in \{\N, \Z, \Q, \R, \CC\}$: the number domain,
  \item $v_i$: the arithmetic value (real/numerator component),
  \item $w_i$: the imaginary/denominator component,
  \item $o_i \in \{+, -, \times, \div, \bmod, \mathbin{**}\}$: the
        arithmetic operation.
\end{itemize}

Extraction proceeds by folding the input through the program:
\begin{equation}
  f_\mathcal{P}(x) = \bigl(\cdots((x \mathbin{o_1^{d_1}} v_1)
  \mathbin{o_2^{d_2}} v_2) \cdots \bigr) \bmod p
\end{equation}
where $o_i^{d_i}$ denotes operation $o_i$ performed in domain $d_i$
with appropriate projections.

\subsection{Program Generation via SHAKE-256}
\label{sec:are:generation}

Given a seed $\sigma \in \{0,1\}^*$ and step count $k$, the program
$\mathcal{P}$ is generated deterministically:

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{ARE Program Generation}
  \label{alg:program-gen}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \REQUIRE Seed $\sigma$, step count $k$
    \ENSURE Program $\mathcal{P} = (s_1, \ldots, s_k)$
    \STATE $\mathit{stream} \gets \mathsf{SHAKE\text{-}256}(\sigma, 34k)$
    \FOR{$i = 1$ to $k$}
      \STATE $d_i \gets \mathit{stream}[34(i\!-\!1)] \bmod 5$
      \STATE $v_i \gets \mathsf{int128}(\mathit{stream}[34(i\!-\!1)\!+\!1 \ldots 34(i\!-\!1)\!+\!16])$
      \STATE $w_i \gets \mathsf{int128}(\mathit{stream}[34(i\!-\!1)\!+\!17 \ldots 34(i\!-\!1)\!+\!32])$
      \STATE $o_i \gets \mathit{stream}[34i\!-\!1] \bmod 6$
    \ENDFOR
    \RETURN $\mathcal{P}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Each step requires exactly 34~bytes: 1~byte for domain selection
($\bmod\,5$), 16~bytes for a signed 128-bit value $v_i$, 16~bytes
for a signed 128-bit imaginary/denominator component $w_i$, and
1~byte for operation selection ($\bmod\,6$).

\subsection{Domain Execution Rules}
\label{sec:are:domains}

\Cref{fig:domains} shows the full $5 \times 6$ domain-operation
matrix; \Cref{tab:domains} defines each domain's arithmetic rules.

\paragraph{Natural ($\N_n$).}
All values are projected to $\{0, \ldots, n-1\}$ via $|v| \bmod n$.
Exponentiation caps the exponent at 64 to prevent computational
explosion: $a^{\min(|b|,64)} \bmod n$.

\paragraph{Integer ($\Z_n$).}
Values are projected into $\{-(n-1), \ldots, n-1\}$ via
$v \bmod (2n-1)$, then shifted if $\geq n$.
Signed exponentiation preserves sign: $\mathrm{sgn}(a) \cdot
|a|^{\min(|b|,64)} \bmod n$.

\paragraph{Rational ($\Q_n$).}
The accumulator is embedded as $\mathrm{acc}/1$; the step value as
$v_i/w_i$ (with $w_i = 0$ replaced by $w_i = 1$).
Arithmetic uses scaled integers to avoid floating-point computation.
The integer part is extracted after each operation.

\paragraph{Real ($\R_n$).}
Fixed-point with $n$-bit fractional precision.
In the pure-Python implementation, $\R_n$ arithmetic is equivalent
to $\Z_n$ arithmetic (the fixed-point scale cancels when both
operands share the same scale).

\paragraph{Complex ($\CC_n$).}
The accumulator is $(a + 0i)$; the step value is $(v_i + w_i \cdot i)$.
After the operation, the real part is projected back to $\Z_n$.
Division uses the conjugate formula:
$(a+0i)/(c+di) = ac/(c^2+d^2)$.

\subsection{Zero-Avoidance Perturbation}
\label{sec:are:zero}

The algebraic fold can encounter absorbing states.
%
Multiplication and modular reduction have zero as a fixed point:
once the accumulator reaches zero, all subsequent multiplicative
or modular steps preserve zero, destroying input entropy.
%
For example, Rational-domain MOD with $v/w$ where $\lfloor v/w
\rfloor \leq 1$ maps any accumulator to~0.

We introduce a deterministic zero-avoidance perturbation.
%
After each step~$i$, if $\mathrm{acc} = 0$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{acc} \gets \bigl((v_i + i + 1) \oplus (x \bmod 256)
  \bigr) \bmod n
\end{equation}
where $v_i$ is the step value, $i$ is the step index, and $x$ is
the original input.
%
This ensures every input traverses a distinct path through the
program. The perturbation is deterministic; given the same seed and
input, extraction is reproducible.

\subsection{Output Expansion via SHA-256 Counter Mode}
\label{sec:are:expansion}

For multi-byte output, the algebraic extraction is composed with
SHA-256 in counter mode.
%
Input data is processed in 16-byte blocks; each block's extracted
value is fed into a running SHA-256 state to produce a 32-byte seed.
%
Counter-mode expansion then generates arbitrarily long output:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{out}_j = \mathsf{SHA\text{-}256}(\mathit{seed} \| j)
  \quad \text{for } j = 0, 1, 2, \ldots
\end{equation}

\subsection{Extended Number Domains}
\label{sec:are:extended}

The five core domains ($\N, \Z, \Q, \R, \CC$) are all commutative
and associative. We extend \are to domains that break these
properties, increasing mixing strength (\Cref{tab:extended}).

\paragraph{Quaternions ($\mathbb{H}$).}
Hamilton's quaternions ($i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1$) are
\emph{non-commutative}: $ij = k$ but $ji = -k$.
%
For \are, this means $\mathrm{acc} \times v \neq v \times
\mathrm{acc}$.
%
SHAKE-256 encodes a left/right multiplication bit per step,
doubling the effective program space.
%
An adversary attempting to invert the program must determine
multiplication direction at each quaternion step.

\paragraph{Octonions ($\mathbb{O}$).}
The octonions, with multiplication defined by the Fano plane, are
both non-commutative and \emph{non-associative}: $(ab)c \neq a(bc)$.
%
By Hurwitz's theorem (1898), $\mathbb{O}$ is the largest normed
division algebra over $\R$, ensuring no zero divisors exist.
%
A sequence of $K$ octonion multiplications admits $C_K =
\binom{2K}{K}/(K+1)$ distinct parenthesizations (Catalan numbers),
none of which can be simplified by regrouping.
%
This makes algebraic shortcut attacks fundamentally harder.

\paragraph{Finite fields $\mathrm{GF}(p^n)$.}
Finite fields provide the strongest formal guarantees.
%
Every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse, and any
invertible operation on a uniformly distributed input produces
uniformly distributed output.
%
This yields a provable per-step min-entropy bound of $\log_2(p^n)$
bits for steps with nonzero operands.
%
$\mathrm{GF}(2^8)$, the AES field, is hardware-accelerated via
the PCLMULQDQ instruction.

\paragraph{$p$-adic numbers ($\Q_p$).}
For each prime $p$, the $p$-adic completion of $\Q$ is defined by
the ultrametric absolute value $|a+b|_p \leq \max(|a|_p, |b|_p)$.
%
Alternating $\R$-domain and $\Q_p$-domain steps forces an
adversary to track the accumulator in two topologically
incompatible metric spaces simultaneously.

\paragraph{Other algebraic structures.}
The \are framework generalizes to any algebraic structure that is closed under at least one of the six operations and admits a bounded projection to the integers.
Examples include split-complex numbers ($j^2 = +1$, useful for hyperbolic mixing) and tropical semirings ($(\min, +)$ algebra, useful for non-Archimedean mixing).
We leave evaluation of these structures to future work.
Cayley--Dickson algebras above $\mathbb{O}$ (sedenions $\mathbb{S}$,
dimension~16) admit \emph{zero divisors}: nonzero $a,b$ with
$ab = 0$. These create absorbing states worse than the zero-collapse
problem in commutative domains. We exclude them from \are.

\subsection{ARE as CSI Conditioner}
\label{sec:are:csi}

A direct application of \are is as a conditioner for WiFi Channel
State Information (CSI) entropy.
%
Prior work~\cite{anon2026csi} extracts only the
least-significant bit (LSB) of each subcarrier phase, then applies
Von~Neumann debiasing, discarding approximately 50\% of the data.
%
\are processes the \emph{full} quantized phase measurement
(all~8~bits per subcarrier) through the algebraic program, reducing
extraction loss from $\sim$50\% to $\sim$15\% while mixing across
subcarriers.
%
This bridges the unilateral CSI entropy pipeline~\cite{anon2026csi} with algebraic extraction, enabling CSI min-entropy to improve from $\sim$5.5 (Von~Neumann LSB-only, as reported in~\cite{anon2026csi}) to an estimated 7.0--7.5~$\bpb$ by utilizing all 8~quantized bits per subcarrier.

\subsection{Distinctness from Known Extractors}
\label{sec:are:novelty}

\Cref{fig:comparison} and \Cref{tab:extractor-comparison} compare
\are against known families.
We argue that \are constitutes a novel extractor family distinct from:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Universal hash functions}~\cite{carter1979universal}:
    compute $h(x) = (ax + b) \bmod p$. \are applies a \emph{sequence}
    of operations across \emph{multiple} domains, not a single
    affine map.

  \item \textbf{Trevisan extractors}~\cite{trevisan2001extractors}:
    use combinatorial designs to select bits for inner extractors.
    \are has no bit-selection phase; it applies algebraic operations
    to the full input.

  \item \textbf{GUV extractors}~\cite{guruswami2009unbalanced}:
    based on list-decodable codes. \are operates over algebraic
    domains, not error-correcting codes.

  \item \textbf{Leftover hash lemma}~\cite{impagliazzo1989one}:
    requires a family of universal hash functions. \are programs are
    generated from a single seed via SHAKE-256, not drawn from a
    universal family.
\end{itemize}

\section{Certified Composition Protocol}
\label{sec:composition}

\subsection{Entropy Source Interface}
\label{sec:composition:interface}

Each entropy source implements a four-method protocol:

\begin{definition}[Entropy Source Protocol]
\label{def:entropy-source}
An entropy source $S$ provides:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $S.\mathsf{name}() \to \mathrm{string}$: source identifier.
  \item $S.\mathsf{read}(n) \to \{0,1\}^{8n}$: read $n$ bytes.
  \item $S.\mathsf{minEntropy}() \to [0, 8]$: estimated $\minentropy$ in
        $\bpb$.
  \item $S.\mathsf{status}() \to \{\textsf{HEALTHY}, \textsf{DEGRADED},
        \textsf{FAILED}\}$: NIST SP~800-90B health classification.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Legacy Provider Adaptation}
\label{sec:composition:adapter}

Existing quantum providers (IBM, Rigetti, QBraid) expose a
different interface (\texttt{get\_entropy(num\_bits)} returning a
bit string). A \texttt{QuantumProviderAdapter} wraps each legacy
provider, running NIST SP~800-90B health tests and min-entropy
estimation on every byte read, so the compositor receives live
status and entropy estimates without requiring provider
modifications.

\subsection{XOR Composition}
\label{sec:composition:xor}

Given active sources $S_1, \ldots, S_m$ (excluding any with
$\mathsf{status} = \textsf{FAILED}$), the compositor reads $n$ bytes
from each and XOR-fuses them:
\begin{equation}
  C = S_1.\mathsf{read}(n) \oplus S_2.\mathsf{read}(n) \oplus
  \cdots \oplus S_m.\mathsf{read}(n)
\end{equation}

The composed min-entropy is conservatively estimated as:
\begin{equation}
  \minentropy(C) \geq \max_{i \in [m]} \minentropy(S_i)
\end{equation}
This follows from the standard bound for XOR of independent sources:
the output is at least as random as the most random input.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig5-health-states}
  \caption{Health monitoring state machine. Sources transition
    between HEALTHY, DEGRADED, and FAILED based on NIST SP~800-90B
    test results. Only FAILED sources are excluded from composition.}
  \label{fig:health}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Health Monitoring and Source Classification}
\label{sec:composition:health}

Each source's health is continuously assessed using NIST
SP~800-90B~\cite{nist2018sp80090b} repetition count and adaptive
proportion tests (\Cref{fig:health}).
A source is classified as:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsf{HEALTHY}: all tests pass.
  \item \textsf{DEGRADED}: anomalies detected; source included with
        logged warning.
  \item \textsf{FAILED}: failure rate exceeds 1\%; source excluded
        from composition.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Graceful Degradation}
\label{sec:composition:degradation}

When a source transitions to \textsf{FAILED}:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The source is excluded from the next composition.
  \item The reported $\minentropy$ bound decreases to reflect only
        contributing sources.
  \item The provenance certificate records the degradation.
  \item If fewer than $\mathsf{min\_sources}$ remain non-FAILED,
        composition raises an error (no silent fallback).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Merkle-Tree Provenance Certificates}
\label{sec:provenance}

\subsection{Provenance Record Format}
\label{sec:provenance:record}

Each source contribution generates a provenance record with canonical
encoding:
\begin{equation}
  r = \mathsf{name} \| \mathsf{minEntropy} \| \mathsf{status} \|
  \mathsf{bytes} \| \mathsf{timestamp} \| \mathsf{sha256}
\end{equation}
where $\|$ denotes pipe-separated UTF-8 encoding.
%
Floating-point values are formatted to 6 decimal places for
deterministic serialization.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig4-merkle-tree}
  \caption{Merkle-tree provenance certificate for three-source
    composition. Each provenance record is hashed as a leaf; the
    root hash $\rho$ serves as the certificate identifier. Odd nodes
    (here, $\ell_3$) are duplicated before pairing.}
  \label{fig:merkle}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Merkle Tree Construction}
\label{sec:provenance:merkle}

\Cref{fig:merkle} illustrates the tree structure.
The provenance certificate is a standard binary Merkle tree:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Each record $r_i$ is serialized and hashed as a leaf:
        $\ell_i = \mathsf{SHA\text{-}256}(r_i)$.
  \item Pairs of leaves are recursively combined:
        $h_{i,j} = \mathsf{SHA\text{-}256}(\ell_i \| \ell_j)$.
  \item Odd-numbered layers duplicate the last node.
  \item The root hash $\rho$ is the certificate identifier.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Verification}
\label{sec:provenance:verification}

Verification recomputes the Merkle root from the stored records and
checks that it matches $\rho$.
%
This confirms that no record has been added, removed, or modified
since certificate creation.
%
An auditor with the certificate can independently verify the
composition's provenance without access to the original entropy data.

\section{Security Analysis}
\label{sec:security}

\subsection{ARE Extraction Guarantee}
\label{sec:security:are}

% NOTE: This is a proof sketch until formally verified.
% Label explicitly per zero-hallucination protocol.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\columnwidth]{figures/fig10-security-game}
  \caption{Security game for \are extraction. The adversary
    distinguishes real extraction output from uniform random via

\paragraph{Two-layer extraction architecture.}
We stress that \are is \emph{not} a standalone extractor.
%
Empirical measurement (\Cref{tab:baseline}) shows that the
algebraic fold alone produces only 122~distinct output values
from 10,000 sequential inputs (5~domains) and fails the
$\chi^2$ test.
%
The output quality of the full \are pipeline comes from the
composition of two layers:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The algebraic fold, which provides input-dependent,
    seed-parameterized mixing across heterogeneous number domains.
  \item SHA-256 counter-mode expansion, which amplifies the fold's
    output into near-uniform bytes.
\end{enumerate}

This two-layer design provides \textbf{defense in depth}: if
SHA-256 is compromised (collision, random oracle model failure),
the algebraic fold still provides mixing that depends on both the
seed and the input.
%
If the algebraic fold is weak for a particular input, SHA-256
still produces pseudorandom output.
%
Neither layer alone suffices; together they provide both algebraic
diversity and cryptographic uniformity.

%

(a)~a second, algebraically distinct mixing layer;

(c)~provable per-step uniformity in the GF domain

and (d)~a framework for incorporating non-commutative and

\paragraph{Two-layer extraction theorem.}
Rather than proving the general ARE extraction bound (which remains open for mixed-domain programs), we prove security of the \emph{two-layer pipeline} directly.

\begin{theorem}[Two-Layer Extraction, ROM]
\label{thm:two-layer}
Model SHA-256 as a random oracle $\mathcal{H}$.
Let $X$ be an entropy source and $Y = \mathrm{fold}_{\mathcal{P}}(X)$ the ARE fold output with $\minentropy(Y) \geq m$.
Then any adversary making at most $q$ queries to $\mathcal{H}$ distinguishes $\mathcal{H}(Y \| 0), \ldots, \mathcal{H}(Y \| L{-}1)$ from $L \times 256$ uniform bits with advantage at most $(q + L) \cdot 2^{-m}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
In the ROM, $\mathcal{H}(Y \| c)$ is uniform for each counter $c$ unless some query to $\mathcal{H}$ hits an input with the same $Y$ prefix.
The probability of any single query matching $Y$ is at most $\max_y \Pr[Y = y] = 2^{-\minentropy(Y)} \leq 2^{-m}$.
By a union bound over $q$ adversarial queries and $L$ counter evaluations, the distinguishing advantage is at most $(q + L) \cdot 2^{-m}$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Concrete security bounds.}
From \Cref{tab:baseline}, the 9-domain fold achieves $\minentropy \geq 4.18$~bpb on 256-byte inputs, giving $m \geq 4.18 \times 256 = 1070$~bits.
For $q = 2^{128}$ and $L = 8$ blocks: advantage $\leq 2^{128-1070} = 2^{-942}$.
Even the 5-domain fold ($m = 0.95 \times 256 = 243$~bits) gives advantage $\leq 2^{128-243} = 2^{-115}$, exceeding 128-bit security.

This theorem separates the two concerns: (1)~the fold's min-entropy preservation, which we \emph{measure empirically} on real entropy pools, and (2)~the SHA-256 expansion, which we \emph{prove} in the ROM.
The open problem is bounding $\minentropy(Y)$ analytically from $\minentropy(X)$ for arbitrary mixed-domain programs; the GF-domain case (\Cref{thm:gf-uniform}) provides the starting point.

\begin{claim}[ARE Min-Entropy Preservation, Open]
\label{claim:are-extraction}
For a random \are program $\mathcal{P}$ of $k$ steps over mixed domains and a source $X$ with $\minentropy(X) \geq k'$, bound $\minentropy(\mathrm{fold}_{\mathcal{P}}(X))$ as a function of $k$, $k'$, and the domain composition of $\mathcal{P}$.
\end{claim}

\begin{remark}
The main difficulty is that non-bijective operations (MOD, EXP in non-GF domains) can reduce min-entropy by a domain-dependent amount per step.
For GF-only programs, \Cref{thm:gf-uniform} gives exact preservation.
For programs with a GF-step majority, the non-GF steps' collision probability can be bounded empirically (\Cref{tab:collision}), and the total min-entropy loss is additive.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Zero-avoidance perturbation.}
The algebraic fold has absorbing states: zero is a fixed point
under multiplication and modular reduction.

\begin{lemma}[Zero-Avoidance Correctness]
\label{lem:zero-avoid}
The zero-avoidance perturbation (replacing $\mathrm{acc} = 0$ with
a deterministic function of the step index, step value, and
original input) preserves the following properties:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Determinism}: the same seed and input always
    produce the same output.
  \item \textbf{Input sensitivity}: different inputs produce
    different perturbation values (via the $x \bmod 256$ term).
  \item \textbf{Non-degeneracy}: the perturbation value is nonzero
    by construction (fallback to $i+1$ if XOR yields zero).
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Properties (1) and (3) follow directly from the perturbation
formula: $\mathrm{acc} \gets ((v_i + i + 1) \oplus (x \bmod 256))
\bmod n$, with the $i+1$ fallback if the result is zero.
%
Property (2) holds because the $x \bmod 256$ term ensures that
distinct inputs $x \neq x'$ with $x \not\equiv x' \pmod{256}$
produce distinct perturbation values at the same step.
%
For inputs where $x \equiv x' \pmod{256}$, the perturbation is
identical, but the fold output may still differ due to earlier
non-zero steps.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Extended Domain Security Arguments}
\label{sec:security:extended}

\paragraph{Non-commutativity ($\mathbb{H}$).}
Each quaternion multiplication step doubles the adversary's search
space: $\mathrm{acc} \times v$ and $v \times \mathrm{acc}$ produce
different results. For $m$ quaternion steps in a $k$-step program,
the program space grows by a factor of $2^m$.

\paragraph{Non-associativity ($\mathbb{O}$).}
For $K$ consecutive octonion multiplications, there are $C_K =
\binom{2K}{K}/(K+1)$ distinct parenthesizations.
%
Standard algebraic simplification (factoring, regrouping) fails;
the adversary must evaluate each parenthesization individually.
%
For $K=8$: $C_8 = 1430$ distinct evaluation orders.

\paragraph{$\mathrm{GF}(p^n)$: provable per-step uniformity.}

\begin{theorem}[GF Per-Step Uniformity]
\label{thm:gf-uniform}
Let $\mathbb{F} = \mathrm{GF}(p^n)$ and let $X$ be uniformly
distributed over $\mathbb{F}^* = \mathbb{F} \setminus \{0\}$.
%
For any fixed $a \in \mathbb{F}^*$, the output $Y = aX$ is
uniformly distributed over $\mathbb{F}^*$.
%
Therefore, each GF multiplication step with nonzero operands
preserves $\log_2(p^n - 1)$ bits of min-entropy exactly.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The map $\varphi_a: x \mapsto ax$ is a bijection on
$\mathbb{F}^*$ because $a \neq 0$ implies $\varphi_a$ has inverse
$\varphi_{a^{-1}}$ (every nonzero element has a multiplicative
inverse in a finite field).
%
For any $y \in \mathbb{F}^*$, there exists exactly one
$x = a^{-1}y$ such that $\varphi_a(x) = y$.
%
Since $X$ is uniform over $\mathbb{F}^*$:
\[
  \Pr[Y = y] = \Pr[X = a^{-1}y] = \frac{1}{|\mathbb{F}^*|}
  = \frac{1}{p^n - 1}
\]
for every $y \in \mathbb{F}^*$, so $Y$ is uniform.
%
The same argument applies to addition (bijection via
$x \mapsto x + a$) and division by nonzero elements.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:gf-chain}
A sequence of $k$ GF steps with nonzero operands on uniform input
preserves $\log_2(p^n - 1)$ bits of min-entropy.
%
For $\mathrm{GF}(2^8)$: each step preserves $\log_2(255) \approx
7.99$ bits, which is the maximum achievable for 8-bit output.
\end{corollary}

This is the strongest formal guarantee among all \are domains.
%
It provides a concrete path toward proving extraction bounds:
if a sufficient fraction of steps in a mixed-domain program are
GF steps, the overall min-entropy loss can be bounded by the
non-GF steps.
%
We leave the general mixed-domain bound as an open problem.

\subsection{Composition Security}
\label{sec:security:composition}

The XOR composition inherits the standard security guarantee: if at
least one source is uncompromised and independent from the others, the
output retains at least the uncompromised source's min-entropy.

\subsection{Provenance Integrity}
\label{sec:security:provenance}

The Merkle-tree certificate provides:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tamper evidence}: modifying any record changes the
        root hash.
  \item \textbf{Completeness}: all contributing sources are recorded.
  \item \textbf{Non-repudiation}: the certificate timestamp binds
        the composition to a specific time.
\end{itemize}

Certificate integrity reduces to the collision resistance of SHA-256.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The algebraic fold alone does not produce uniform output
(\Cref{tab:baseline}); the SHA-256 expansion layer is essential.
%
This means the output uniformity guarantee reduces to SHA-256's
security in the random oracle model, with the algebraic fold
providing a second, structurally independent mixing layer.
%
The GF domain is the only one with a proven per-step uniformity
bound (\Cref{thm:gf-uniform}); the security contributions of
$\mathbb{H}$, $\mathbb{O}$, and $\Q_p$ domains remain
conjectured.
%
The 9-domain programs have a 27\% collision rate in the fold
(\Cref{tab:collision}), higher than 5-domain programs (4\%),
suggesting that extended domains introduce projection losses that
partly offset their algebraic benefits.

\paragraph{Scope.}
Our benchmarks use a single machine (Apple Silicon, Python~3.11).
A Rust implementation would likely achieve 10$\times$ higher
throughput.
%
The CSI entropy pool (9\,KB) is too small for statistical
significance; larger deployments with ESP32-S3 hardware are needed.
%
We did not run the official NIST SP~800-90B \texttt{ea\_non\_iid}
tool (not installed); our statistical tests (chi-squared, runs,
serial, autocorrelation) are consistent with but not equivalent to
the full NIST assessment.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the Certified Heterogeneous Entropy (\che) framework,
a two-layer extraction architecture combining seed-parameterized
algebraic mixing (\are) with SHA-256 expansion, Merkle-tree
provenance certificates, and graceful degradation with accurate
min-entropy bounds.
%
The \are construction operates over nine number domains, from
classical ($\N, \Z, \Q, \R, \CC$) to extended
($\mathbb{H}, \mathbb{O}, \mathrm{GF}(p^n), \Q_p$), each
contributing distinct algebraic properties.
%
We proved per-step uniformity for the GF domain
(\Cref{thm:gf-uniform}), identified the general extraction bound
as an open problem, and showed empirically that the two-layer
pipeline passes all statistical tests on 6.8\,MB of real quantum
entropy.
%
The provenance certificates satisfy DORA Art.~7 requirements for
cryptographic key lifecycle management in EU-regulated financial
institutions.

\paragraph{Open problems.}
(1)~Bound the collision probability of the algebraic fold for
mixed-domain programs, building on fuzzy extractor
theory~\cite{dodis2008fuzzy}; the GF-only case is near-bijective
(98\% unique, \Cref{tab:collision}) and may extend to programs with
a GF-step majority.
(2)~Prove that non-commutativity ($\mathbb{H}$) and
non-associativity ($\mathbb{O}$) increase inversion resistance
beyond search-space arguments.
(3)~Extend the framework to device-independent quantum entropy
certification~\cite{aaronson2025certified}.
(4)~Integrate with NIST SP~800-90C RBG
constructions~\cite{nist2025sp80090c}.
